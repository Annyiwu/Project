Different Settings


Prone Setting: The most common and popular type of setting is prong setting, usually with either 4 or 6 prongs that are bent over the girdle of the gemstone or diamond. Benefiting from having a high position, the gemstone will receive maximum exposure to light from top to bottom and present its allure. This type of setting can be used for all kinds of faceted stones. It allows the stone to obtain the right height, be completely straight, centered and secure; many prefer this setting type for their engagement rings, stud earrings or tennis bracelets.

Channel Setting：As probably the most beautifully designed gem for rings, it is a method whereby stones are suspended between two bars or strips of metal, called channels. Instead of each stone being held by an individual set of prongs, this style aligns several gems girdle-to-girdle in a row by hammering the upper sides of the channel walls. Channel settings can be used for a variety of faceted stones - round, brilliant, princess cut, emerald cut, baguettes etc.

Bezel Setting: It is essentially a metal band that wraps around a stone, which is typically used to mount flat-backed stones and round shaped diamonds. A bezel setting is stronger and more secure than a prong or channel setting; this type of setting could also allow the stones to have edges, or can be molded into any shape to accommodate the stone. However, since bezel settings does not provide as much light through the gemstone, the stones' brilliance are reduced.

Pave Setting: It is a modified version of prong setting but is one of the most difficult gem settings to create. Derived from the French word "Pave" meaning to be paved, this setting has the diamonds set on the metal with holes that are slightly smaller than the gems’ girdles drilled into the metal. The stones are then placed inside these holes, and are pulled up from the metal and pushed against the diamond. Such settings with tiny diamonds create a continuous surface of radiance and shimmer and the stunning effect of setting type is thus preferred for engagement rings.

Invisible Setting: It is one of the most difficult setting methods, and is used to create the illusion of larger diamonds. In this setting, the stones are positioned in such a manner that metal is not visible in between stones, giving the appearance of an uninterrupted and continuous surface.


Unique Settings by Brands

Tiffany Setting

As its classic setting for the iconic engagement ring, Tiffany's setting was simple: by raising a ring's center diamond using six evenly-spaced claws to maximize the light that travels through it.

Van Cleef &Arpels Mystery Setting

First used in 1933, the Mystery Set technique is extremely intricating and produced rarely. The setting allows the stones to be displayed without any visible mounting by delicately inserting each faceted stone onto thin gold rails less than two-tenths of a millimetre thick. Believe it or not, a single clip takes no less than 300 hours of work.

Cartier Trembling Setting

Inspired by the cosmos, the Trembling Setting is a miniaturized version of its original setting technique.

Chopard Magical Setting

Chopard’s newly-unveiled and ingenious setting technique, Magical Setting, minimizes the amount of metal between the stones. This exclusive technique allows the maximum amount of light to reflect between the piece’s precious gemstones, and the center stone is set in barely-there claws, which makes it look as though each jewel hangs mid-air.

Technology development


3D Printing

3D printing technology and computer-aided design (CAD) software has upgraded the world of designer jewelry and has added more precision to the carving and polishing of gemstones. For instance, the CAD software creates a 3D digital file of any customized jewelry design, and then with 3D printing technology, a thermoplastic wax mould is obtained. This technique helps to visualize the custom design and reduce the additional cost to further modify the jewelry after finished.


Serenity Technologies: The New Coating Technology

Serenity Technologies use multiple micro-thin coatings of various compositions to produce a variety of colors on diamonds, including intense blue, green, yellow, and orange to pink to purple-pink. This technology was first initiated by Epelboym in 2000: during which the orange-treated diamonds were suspected of being coated with a silica film doped with gold.
